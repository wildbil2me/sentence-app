# Product Naming Brief

**For:** naming/branding research
**Working title:** "Grammar Lab" (placeholder — assume it is available to change)
**Date:** 2026-07-21

---

## 1. One-line description

A browser-based tool for teaching sentence structure: a teacher pastes any
paragraph, color-labels its words, phrases, and clauses, projects the breakdown
one sentence at a time, and hands the same lesson to students as a
self-checking quiz.

## 2. What it actually does

The product is a single web app with three modes over one shared lesson.

**Edit (teacher).** Paste any paragraph. It is split into sentences
automatically. The teacher drags across words — or clicks a single word — and
picks a label from a palette. Labels come from four "teaching levels":

- **Parts of Speech** — noun, verb, adjective, adverb, pronoun, conjunction,
  preposition, interjection, determiner
- **Sentence Parts** — subject, predicate, object, complement, appositive
- **Phrases** — noun, verb, prepositional, appositive, absolute, and the
  verbal phrases (gerund, participial, infinitive)
- **Clauses** — independent and dependent (relative, adverbial, noun)

Every one of those drills down. *Noun* opens into common, proper, collective,
abstract, concrete, possessive. *Subject* opens into simple, complete,
compound, understood. About 87 labels in total, each with its own color, plain
definition, and worked example. A per-lesson "Essential only" switch trims the
palette to the classroom-core set so a beginner class is not staring at
"emphatic pronoun."

Each sentence can also be tagged on two independent axes: **structure**
(simple / compound / complex / compound-complex) and **purpose** (declarative /
interrogative / imperative / exclamatory).

Any label can carry the teacher's own **note** — the thing they actually want
to say when they get to that word.

**Present (teacher).** The same lesson, one sentence at a time, sized for a
projector. Crucially, **every level starts hidden**. The teacher toggles levels
on to build the analysis up in front of the class — first the parts of speech,
then the sentence parts, then the clauses — instead of revealing a finished
diagram. Clicking any label shows its definition, an example, and the teacher's
note. Arrow keys advance.

**Practice (student).** The same lesson auto-generates a quiz. No extra
authoring. Question types: "what is this highlighted word?" (multiple choice),
"select the direct object" (drag across the words in the sentence), and "what
is the structure/purpose of this sentence?" Wrong answers are drawn from the
same grammatical family as the right one, so the distractors are the ones
genuinely worth ruling out. Instant feedback, a streak counter, and a results
screen listing what is worth reviewing.

A built-in library ships with ready-made labeled passages, including literary
ones — the *Romeo and Juliet* prologue, the closing lines of *The Great
Gatsby*, the creation scene from *Frankenstein*, Stoker's description of the
Count. Teachers copy one and edit it.

## 3. The core idea, in one sentence

Grammar instruction usually means either a static textbook diagram or a
worksheet of someone else's sentences. This turns **any text the teacher
already cares about** — this week's novel, a student's own paragraph — into a
live, layered, projectable analysis *and* the practice that follows from it, in
one pass.

## 4. What makes it distinct

- **Bring your own text.** Not a fixed exercise bank. The teacher's material,
  labeled in minutes.
- **Progressive reveal.** The analysis is built up live in front of a class,
  not shown finished.
- **Author once, quiz free.** Labeling the passage *is* authoring the quiz.
- **Depth on a dial.** Same tool serves "find the noun" and "identify the
  absolute phrase," via the drill-down palette and the Essential/Advanced
  switch.
- **Zero friction.** No install, no server, no accounts, no student logins.
  Opens as a web page; lessons save in the browser and move between machines as
  a single exported file.

## 5. Who it is for

- **Primary:** middle and high school English/ELA teachers (roughly grades
  6–12), teaching to standards like Common Core 9–10/11–12 and Texas TEKS.
- **Secondary:** homeschool educators; ESL/EFL instructors; writing-center and
  first-year-composition tutors; self-directed adult learners brushing up.
- **The student** is a user but not the buyer — they meet the product in
  Practice mode, on whatever device the classroom has.

## 6. Tone and positioning

The tone the product already has: precise, uncondescending, a little playful.
It treats grammar as something with visible structure worth looking at —
closer to a microscope or an X-ray than to a drill sergeant. Color-coded,
diagrammatic, hands-on. It is emphatically **not** a gamified points-and-badges
app, and not a "grammar checker" (it never corrects the user's writing — it
analyzes text the teacher chose).

Words that feel right: *structure, layers, anatomy, breakdown, close reading,
build it up, see it.*
Words that feel wrong: *fix, correct, error, proofread, mastery points.*

## 7. Naming constraints and cautions

- **Avoid "grammar checker" adjacency.** The category is crowded with
  correction tools (Grammarly and its many imitators). A name that reads as
  "checks your writing" actively miscommunicates what this does.
- The working title **"Grammar Lab" is likely too generic** — the phrase is
  widely used by existing sites, worksheets, and school programs. Treat it as a
  description of the concept, not a candidate.
- Should read as classroom-safe and school-procurement-safe: pronounceable by a
  teacher out loud to a room, printable on a handout, no ambiguity in a school
  web filter.
- Ideally works internationally / for ESL contexts; avoid US-only idiom.

## 8. Deliverable requested

Candidate names with: rationale, a read on category confusion (especially vs.
grammar-correction tools), domain and app-store availability, and a rough
trademark-conflict check in the education software space. A short tagline
alongside each candidate would help.

## 9. Open questions the namer may want answered

These are not yet decided and may affect the naming direction:

- Is this free/open, freemium, or a paid school-license product?
- Is the audience primarily US, or international from the start?
- Is a sibling product planned? (A companion tool for punctuation, usage errors
  such as run-ons and dangling modifiers, and verb tense/voice is scoped but
  deliberately out of this app — if it ships, the two may need a shared family
  name.)
